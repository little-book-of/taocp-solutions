# 2.5 Dynamic Storage Allocation

This section covers reserve/release policies, fragmentation, buddy systems, and compaction/garbage collection.

### 1 [06]
A free-list allocator must define: block header layout, placement policy, split rule, coalescing rule, and failure behavior.

### 2 [08]
First-fit scans free list and takes first block large enough. It is fast but can create front-of-list fragmentation.

### 3 [08]
Best-fit minimizes immediate leftover but tends to create many tiny unusable fragments; search cost is higher.

### 4 [08]
Next-fit continues scan from previous position; reduces repeated head scans but may degrade locality.

### 5 [08]
Worst-fit preserves large blocks by carving from largest free block; can help some workloads but unstable in others.

### 6 [10]
Boundary tags allow O(1)-neighbor coalescing on release by storing size/state at both block ends.

### 7 [10]
Immediate coalescing merges adjacent free blocks at every `release`; deferred coalescing postpones merging until pressure increases.

### 8 [10]
External fragmentation: enough total free memory exists but not in sufficiently large contiguous blocks.

### 9 [10]
Internal fragmentation: allocated block exceeds request due to granularity/alignment/splitting constraints.

### 10 [12]
A reserve operation should split only if remainder exceeds minimum block size; otherwise hand out whole block.

### 11 [12]
Release operation correctness invariant: union of free and allocated blocks exactly partitions arena, no overlaps or gaps.

### 12 [12]
Compaction removes external fragmentation by relocating live blocks and updating references through relocation map.

### 13 [14]
Garbage collection (mark-sweep):
1. mark reachable nodes from roots,
2. sweep unmarked nodes into free list,
3. optionally clear marks.

### 14 [14]
Reference counting reclaims promptly but fails on cycles unless augmented.

### 15 [14]
Copying collectors compact naturally by moving live objects into to-space; cost proportional to live data, not heap size.

### 16 [16]
Buddy system keeps blocks in powers of two; split/merge are logarithmic and coalescing is fast via buddy-address arithmetic.

### 17 [16]
Buddy coalescing condition: both buddy blocks free and same order. Merge recursively upward.

### 18 [12]
Binary buddy worst-case internal waste per allocation is < 50% of allocated block size.

### 19 [12]
Choosing minimum order too small increases metadata overhead; too large increases internal fragmentation.

### 20 [14]
Segregated free lists (size classes) make allocation near O(1) average for common sizes.

### 21 [14]
Placement policy can be workload-sensitive; no single policy dominates universally.

### 22 [12]
For real-time systems, bounded-time allocators favor fixed-size pools/slabs over variable-size general heaps.

### 23 [16]
Pool allocators eliminate external fragmentation within pool but require good size-class design.

### 24 [16]
Slab allocators add object-cache reuse and constructor/destructor locality benefits.

### 25 [14]
Deferred reclamation can improve throughput by batching coalescing/metadata updates.

### 26 [14]
Relocation requires either precise pointers, tagged pointers, or conservative scanning strategy.

### 27 [12]
A robust allocator exports diagnostics: free-list integrity checks, double-free detection, and leak statistics.

### 28 [14]
Game-state win/loss retrograde allocation tasks can be expressed with predecessor lists plus free-list recycling.

### 29 [12]
Mass erase of linked structures is linear in released nodes; splice whole chain to free list tail for cache-friendly behavior.

### 30 [12]
Amortized analysis: occasional expensive repacks/compactions are acceptable if triggered infrequently by growth thresholds.

### 31 [18]
Fibonacci buddy variants reduce internal waste versus binary buddy but complicate split/merge logic and buddy identification.

### 32 [M20]
Analytical occupancy/distribution questions depend on request-size distribution; derive expected waste from that model.

### 33 [20]
Mark-compact outline:
1. mark live objects,
2. compute new addresses by prefix-sum over live sizes,
3. rewrite pointers,
4. move objects.

### 34 [20]
Naive compactors can be quadratic if each moved object triggers full rescans; linear-time compactors use forwarding tables.

### 35 [14]
Compared with §2.2.2 table reallocation: dynamic allocation generalizes to many object lifetimes and noncontiguous structures, at cost of metadata and fragmentation management.

### 36 [22]
Seating/packing analogues model fragmentation constraints; constructive placement strategies can guarantee future fit under restricted arrivals.

### 37 [16]
Counterexamples with one fewer slot show sharp thresholds: a policy feasible for `n` may fail for `n-1` due to parity/modular packing constraints.

### 38 [16]
`N(n,2)`-type formulas count guaranteed capacity under adversarial arrivals of size-1/size-2 requests; derive via extremal packing patterns.

### 39 [M24]
Higher-order asymptotic bounds typically require generating functions/probabilistic combinatorics.

### 40 [M24]
Open problems on exact `N(n,k)` values illustrate sensitivity of online packing guarantees.

### 41 [22]
Buddy-system bound idea: wasted space is dominated by geometric tail of partially filled orders, giving worst-case upper bound proportional to highest order requested.

### 42 [M24]
Advanced bound refinements require detailed state-space analysis of order lists under adversarial request sequences.

### 43 [M24]
Expected-case buddy utilization under random requests is analyzed by Markov-chain/renewal methods.

### 44 [M24]
Extending these bounds to nonbinary buddy families remains mathematically difficult; most practical systems rely on simulation plus empirical tuning.
